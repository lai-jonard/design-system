LawAdvisor AI — UX Guidelines

Version 1.1 · April 2026

A working reference for how LawAdvisor AI surfaces are designed, written, and evaluated. Use it as a spec when building new AI features, and as a checklist when auditing existing ones.

Scope: Sprints 1–10. Our AI is backend-driven — the UI surfaces results rather than exposing the model. The four client-facing AI outputs covered here are:

  • Insights (Sprint 5) — AI-generated cards surfacing legal problems and suggested actions, with severity tags, deadlines, and source references back to document pages.
  • Document analysis (Sprint 6) — key terms and obligations extracted from uploaded documents, entity associations, linked insights.
  • Auto-level analysis — background re-scanning when new documents are uploaded, surfaced via the processing widget.
  • DD Readiness (Sprint 10) — an AI-driven indicator showing how close a founder is to being fundraise-ready, severity-gated with a remaining issues count.

Patterns relevant to future surfaces (chat, agents, drafting) are flagged Deferred so we don't lose track of them.

Sources referenced throughout:

  • The Shape of AI — UX pattern library by Emily Campbell
  • Microsoft Responsible AI Principles
  • IBM Design for AI
  • Google People + AI Guidebook
  • Apple HIG — Machine Learning
  • EU AI Act — high-risk system obligations (relevant as we sell into legal)
  • ICO Guidance on AI and data protection (UK context)

Part 1: Core principles

Six non-negotiables. These are our ethical floor — everything below must respect them.

1.1 Accountability

The AI augments the founder. It never makes the final legal call. Every AI-driven workflow has a human owner on the LawAdvisor side (PM or engineering lead), and every founder can override, dismiss, or escalate any AI output.

In practice:

  • Every Insight, extracted term, and DD Readiness signal must be dismissable or correctable by the founder.
  • Feedback signals ("this is wrong", "this isn't relevant") flow back to the product team, not just engineering telemetry.
  • Internal ownership is documented per AI surface — who reviews failures, who decides when to pull a feature.

Reference: Microsoft Responsible AI — Accountability · IBM Design for AI — Accountability focal area.

1.2 Explainability

A founder must always be able to trace why the AI surfaced something. No AI output appears without a reference trail back to the source.

In practice:

  • Every Insight links to the source document and page.
  • Every extracted term highlights the clause it came from.
  • DD Readiness signals break down into the underlying issues that produced them.
  • When the AI can't explain itself (low confidence, unclear source), say so — don't fabricate a citation.

Reference: Microsoft Responsible AI — Transparency · Google PAIR — Explainability + Trust.

1.3 Fairness & bias minimisation

Legal AI bias disadvantages users by jurisdiction, business type, or practice area. We check for it actively.

In practice:

  • Regular sampling of Insights and DD Readiness outputs across jurisdictions and company structures.
  • Feedback mechanism on every AI surface lets founders flag skewed or irrelevant output.
  • Training/tuning datasets are documented — we know what went in.

Reference: Microsoft — Fairness Checklist (AIF360 related) · ICO — Fairness in AI.

1.4 Data rights & confidentiality

Founders share commercially sensitive material. Data control is a core feature, not a compliance checkbox.

In practice:

  • Uploaded document content is never used to train shared models without explicit firm-level consent.
  • Settings surface what the AI has scanned and lets founders delete it.
  • GDPR + applicable regional regimes (UK GDPR, AU Privacy Act) fully respected.
  • Legal professional privilege implications flagged to legal counsel before any cross-firm feature ships.

Reference: ICO — AI and data protection · EU AI Act Art. 10 (data governance).

1.5 Transparency & disclosure

Founders always know when output is AI-generated. This is especially important because founders might forward our output to investors, lawyers, or counterparties.

In practice:

  • Every AI-generated card, term, or indicator carries a consistent AI label.
  • Action-specific verbs over generic badges ("Risks found by AI", "Terms identified by AI") — see Pattern 2.6.
  • Exports and shares of AI output retain the disclosure.

Reference: Shape of AI — Disclosure · EU AI Act Art. 50 (transparency obligations).

1.6 Human oversight & agency

The AI never closes the loop on a legal issue — the founder does. The AI surfaces; the founder decides.

In practice:

  • No AI action in sprints 1–10 is irreversible.
  • Founders can dismiss, resolve, or escalate any Insight.
  • The auto re-scan process is visible — never silent — and its outputs can be questioned.
  • When we ship actions in the future (filing, sending, committing), friction scales with risk.

Reference: EU AI Act Art. 14 (human oversight) · Microsoft — Human-AI Interaction Guidelines.

Part 2: Patterns we use

Organised by Shape of AI taxonomy. Each pattern has: what it is, how we use it, our copy examples, and a reference link. Patterns not relevant to sprints 1–10 are listed under Deferred at the end.

2.1 Attachments (Tuners)

What it is: Users ground the AI in specific documents rather than relying on general model knowledge. Reduces hallucination.

Reference: Shape of AI — Attachments.

How we use it:

  • Document upload is the primary input to our AI. No Insight exists without an associated document.
  • Upload surface shows what file types are supported, what happens after upload, and where the output will appear.
  • Founders can see every document the AI has ingested in a single place.

Copy examples:

Context ✅
Upload CTA "Add document"
Empty state "No documents yet. Add one to get started."
Processing "We're reviewing your documents."
Post-upload affirmation "Got it. We'll let you know what we find."

Don'ts:

  • Don't say "Submit" or "Upload" on the CTA — "Add document" is our standard.
  • Don't quote time estimates ("This takes ~3 minutes"). Let processing speak for itself via the widget.

2.2 Citations (Governors) — P0

What it is: Every AI claim traces back to the source material. Non-negotiable for any product surfacing legal information.

Reference: Shape of AI — Citations · Google PAIR — Explainability.

How we use it:

  • Every Insight card deep-links to the exact page of the document it references. Not the document — the page.
  • Extracted terms (Document analysis) highlight the specific clause in the source.
  • DD Readiness breaks down into citable issues, each of which cites its document(s).
  • When a citation is missing or ambiguous, we say so explicitly — we don't hide the gap.

Copy examples:

Context ✅ ❌
Citation label "From Founders Agreement, p.4" "Source"
Multi-source "From 2 documents" (click to expand) "Multiple sources"
Missing citation "We found this but couldn't link it to a specific page." (silent)

Design standard:

  • Link format: [Document name, p.X] — always include page number when available.
  • Clicking a citation opens the document at that page with the referenced passage highlighted.
  • Citations are visually subordinate to the insight content but always visible — never behind a hover or tooltip.

Don'ts:

  • Don't surface an Insight with no citation. If we can't cite it, we don't show it.
  • Don't link to "the document" generally — always link to the page.

2.3 Footprints (Trust Builders) — P0

What it is: The audit trail from an AI output back through the steps and data that produced it.

Reference: Shape of AI — Footprints.

How we use it:

  • Every Insight shows: what document triggered it, what clause, what the AI identified, what severity was assigned, when it was generated.
  • Document analysis shows what was extracted and from where.
  • The processing widget shows what's being analysed right now and what's finished.

Copy examples:

Context ✅
Footprint header "Why we flagged this"
Trigger description "Spotted in your Shareholders Agreement uploaded on 12 April."
Generation timestamp "Found on 15 April."

Design standard:

  • Footprints are reviewable (a log), not just a live trace.
  • When the source document is deleted, dependent Insights are either removed or clearly marked "source no longer available."
  • Founders can export their footprint trail for any Insight (useful when sharing with a lawyer).

2.4 Caveat (Trust Builders) — P0

What it is: Contextual notes about the AI's limitations, placed at the point of decision-making — not in a footer.

Reference: Shape of AI — Caveat · Google PAIR — Mental Models.

How we use it:

  • Every Insight severity has calibrated caveat language (see Pattern 2.9 — Severity Signaling).
  • DD Readiness carries a caveat about what it does and doesn't cover.
  • Extracted terms carry a caveat that they need human review before being treated as authoritative.

Voice note: Caveats follow the LawAdvisor voice — calm, plain-English, guidance not authority. The doc we inherited from suggests "precise, measured, avoid humour" — we don't adopt that register. It sounds like a barrister. Ours sounds like a capable co-founder.

Copy examples:

Context ✅ (ours) ❌ (barrister register)
Insight footer "Worth checking with your lawyer before acting on this." "This output does not constitute legal advice."
DD Readiness "This gives you a rough picture — your lawyer or investor may weigh things differently." "Readiness scores are indicative only and should not be relied upon."
Extracted terms "Have a quick read to make sure we got this right." "Extracted content should be verified for accuracy."
Jurisdiction gap "We didn't find anything specific to [jurisdiction] — worth double-checking." "Jurisdictional variance may apply."

Don'ts:

  • Don't bury caveats in a footer disclaimer. Place them where the decision happens.
  • Don't use "may not" hedging ("This may or may not be accurate"). It undermines everything.
  • Don't over-caveat. If every card has three warnings, founders stop reading them.

2.5 Disclosure (Trust Builders) — P0

What it is: Consistent labelling of AI-generated content across the product.

Reference: Shape of AI — Disclosure · EU AI Act Art. 50.

How we use it:

  • Action-specific verbs rather than a generic "AI" badge.
  • Disclosure persists on export, share, and print.
  • Every AI surface has a visible sparkle icon (see Pattern 2.7 — Iconography).

Copy examples:

Surface ✅ ❌
Insights section header "Risks we found" with sparkle icon "AI Insights"
Extracted terms "Terms we pulled out" with sparkle icon "AI-extracted Terms"
DD Readiness "Your DD readiness" with sparkle icon "AI-generated Readiness Score"
Processing widget "We're reviewing your documents" "AI is analysing"

Pattern: We say "we" (the LawAdvisor product) rather than "AI" as the actor, but the sparkle icon makes the AI provenance unambiguous. This matches our voice ("smart co-founder who handles the legal side") without hiding what's happening.

Don'ts:

  • Don't use "AI" as a user-facing noun if "we" + sparkle icon conveys the same thing.
  • Don't remove disclosure on exports or shares. If a founder forwards an Insights summary, the AI label travels with it.

2.6 Consent & data ownership (Trust Builders) — P0

What it is: Clear, visible controls for what the AI accesses, retains, and uses.

Reference: Shape of AI — Consent · ICO — AI and data protection.

How we use it:

  • Consent wording appears at document upload — not buried in ToS.
  • Settings area shows: documents the AI has scanned, data retention state, and whether content is used for model improvement.
  • Delete is honoured — not just flagged. When a founder deletes a document, dependent AI outputs are removed.

Copy examples:

Context ✅
Upload consent "We'll review this to find risks specific to your business. You can delete it anytime."
Settings header "What we have on file"
Delete confirmation "Delete this document? Anything we found in it will also be removed."

Don'ts:

  • Don't pre-tick consent boxes.
  • Don't treat deletion as "mark as deleted." Actually remove the content from the AI's working set.

2.7 Iconography (Identifiers)

What it is: Consistent visual signalling of AI-powered content across the interface.

Reference: Shape of AI — Iconography.

How we use it:

  • One AI icon (sparkle) used consistently — on Insight cards, extracted term chips, DD Readiness, processing widget.
  • Same colour token across all instances (see design system).
  • Icon appears at the feature boundary, not on every element inside.

Pattern:

  • ✅ Sparkle icon next to "Risks we found" section header.
  • ❌ Sparkle icon on every individual Insight card within that section.

Don'ts:

  • Don't introduce a second AI icon for different features. One icon, consistent.
  • Don't animate the icon ambiently. It's a marker, not a distraction.

2.8 Feedback loop — P0, new pattern

What it is: A way for founders to signal when the AI got it wrong. Not in the Shape of AI taxonomy but essential for our context.

Reference: Google PAIR — Feedback + Control · Microsoft — Human-AI Interaction Guideline 15.

How we use it:

  • Every Insight card has a "Not relevant" / "This isn't right" action.
  • Feedback is captured in a structured way (reason codes + free text) and surfaced to the product team.
  • Founders get a quick acknowledgement — they don't hear silence.

Copy examples:

Context ✅
Feedback action "Mark as not relevant"
Reason picker "Why? (helps us improve)" with options: "Doesn't apply to us", "We've already handled this", "This is wrong", "Other"
Acknowledgement "Thanks — we'll use this to improve."

Don'ts:

  • Don't use a thumbs-up/down alone without a reason code — it tells us something is wrong but not what.
  • Don't silently swallow feedback. Acknowledge it.

2.9 Severity signaling — P0, new pattern

What it is: How we express the severity and confidence of an AI-surfaced issue. Our Insights carry severity tags and DD Readiness is severity-gated, so this needs a standard.

Reference: Google PAIR — Confidence · Apple HIG — Machine Learning (calibrating confidence).

How we use it:

  • Three severity levels: High (needs attention soon), Medium (worth reviewing), Low (informational).
  • Severity is calibrated by: potential impact × confidence that the issue applies.
  • Severity drives visual treatment (colour, icon) and caveat wording.
  • When the AI is unsure of severity, it defaults to Medium — not High.

Copy examples:

Severity Label Caveat example
High "Needs attention" "Worth sorting this before you raise."
Medium "Worth a look" "Have a read when you've got a moment."
Low "For your awareness" "Nothing urgent — just so you know."

Don'ts:

  • Don't use "Critical" / "Urgent" / "Warning" — urgency erodes trust (per voice rules).
  • Don't mix severity language with deadline language. A High-severity item isn't the same as a 7-day deadline — both can exist independently.
  • Don't show raw confidence percentages ("78% confident") — they falsely suggest precision.

2.10 Re-scan transparency — P0, new pattern

What it is: Our auto-level analysis re-scans existing documents when new ones are uploaded. Silent re-scans are a trust landmine — founders see issue counts change without explanation.

Reference: Google PAIR — System Behaviour Updates · Shape of AI — Memory (related but not exact).

How we use it:

  • The processing widget surfaces what's being re-scanned, not just "analysing."
  • When an Insight is added/removed/changed as a result of re-scan, the founder sees a change indicator.
  • History of re-scans is visible in the footprint trail.

Copy examples:

Context ✅
Re-scan in progress "We're taking another look now that you've added [Document]."
New issue surfaced by re-scan "Found after reviewing [Document]."
Issue resolved by re-scan "Resolved after your [Document] was added."

Don'ts:

  • Don't change issue counts silently. Always show what changed and why.
  • Don't lump re-scan into a generic "processing" state. Be specific about what triggered it.

2.11 Aggregate confidence (DD Readiness) — P0, new pattern

What it is: DD Readiness rolls up many underlying signals into one indicator. A single number ("72% ready") implies precision we don't have. We need a standard for how to express aggregate AI judgments.

Reference: Google PAIR — Summary Metrics · Apple HIG — Machine Learning (expressing uncertainty).

How we use it:

  • DD Readiness is expressed as a band with a remaining issue count, not a raw percentage.
  • Bands: Just getting started → Making progress → Almost there → Ready.
  • The indicator always shows: band, count of remaining issues, and one-line framing.
  • Founders can drill down into the issues that make up the band.

Copy examples:

Band Framing
Just getting started "You're early — let's work through the essentials first."
Making progress "Good progress. [N] things still need sorting."
Almost there "Nearly ready. [N] things left to clear up."
Ready "You're in good shape."

Don'ts:

  • Don't show a raw percentage. It invites investor conversations like "you're only 72%?" that the AI can't defend.
  • Don't use "score" language ("Readiness score: B+"). This isn't a grade.
  • Don't conceal the underlying issues. The band is a shortcut, not a black box.

Part 4: Audit checklist

Use this to evaluate any existing or new AI flow. Pass = the answer is a confident yes. Anything else is an action item.

Insights

  • [ ] Every Insight card carries a sparkle icon and action-specific disclosure language
  • [ ] Every Insight links to a specific document page (not the document broadly)
  • [ ] When a citation can't be resolved, the card says so rather than hiding the gap
  • [ ] Severity uses our three-band system (High / Medium / Low) with calibrated caveat copy
  • [ ] Founders can dismiss or mark-as-resolved every Insight
  • [ ] Every Insight has a "Not relevant" / "This isn't right" feedback action with reason codes
  • [ ] The footprint trail shows why the Insight was flagged, when, and from what
  • [ ] Caveat copy sounds like a calm co-founder, not a barrister (see voice test below)
  • [ ] Exported or shared Insights retain their AI disclosure

Document analysis

  • [ ] Extracted terms carry sparkle icon and action-specific disclosure
  • [ ] Each extracted term highlights the specific clause in the source document
  • [ ] Terms carry a "have a quick read to make sure we got this right" caveat
  • [ ] Founders can flag an extraction as wrong, with reason codes
  • [ ] Deleting the source document removes dependent extractions

Auto-level analysis (processing widget)

  • [ ] The widget tells founders what document triggered the re-scan, not just "processing"
  • [ ] Issue count changes are accompanied by a visible reason (new finding / resolved finding)
  • [ ] Founders can see a history of re-scans in the footprint trail
  • [ ] Re-scan does not silently change severity or citations without indication

DD Readiness

  • [ ] Expressed as a band + remaining issue count, not a raw percentage
  • [ ] Band label and framing follow our four-band standard
  • [ ] Founders can drill into the underlying issues
  • [ ] Caveat explains that investors / lawyers may weigh things differently
  • [ ] Sparkle icon and disclosure present

Cross-cutting

  • [ ] Upload flow shows consent wording in-context (not buried in ToS)
  • [ ] Settings area shows "what we have on file" and allows deletion
  • [ ] Deleted documents are actually removed from the AI's working set within a defined window
  • [ ] All caveat copy passes the voice gut-check (see below)

Voice gut-check (from UX writing skill)

For every piece of AI-surface copy:

  1. Would a calm, capable co-founder say this?
  2. Does it add stress, distance, or authority?
  3. Can a founder scan it in 2 seconds?
  4. Is there a next step if something needs action?
  5. Does it sound like a legal document or system alert?

If 2 or 5 are yes, rewrite. If 1, 3, 4 are no, rewrite.
