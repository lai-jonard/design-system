Copy & Capitalisation

LawAdvisor uses a two-register capitalisation system. The core question for any string is: does this copy name a product surface or control, or does it explain something to the user? Names get Title Case. Explanations get sentence case.

The rule

One question decides the register for every string in the product:

Does this copy name a product surface or control? → Title Case.
Does this copy explain something to the user? → Sentence case.

This is a deliberate divergence from design systems that recommend sentence case everywhere. LawAdvisor's vocabulary includes genuine proper nouns — legal documents, commercial product areas — where Title Case is standard professional practice. Forcing sentence case on "Cap Table" or "Due Diligence Pack" would feel wrong in a legal context. The two-register system formalises what the product already does naturally.

Title Case

Use when the copy names a product surface, legal artefact, or interactive control. Capitalise every principal word. Articles, prepositions, and conjunctions (a, an, the, for, and, but, of, in) stay lowercase unless they open the label.

ElementExample
Navigation itemsGovernance, Employment, Fundraising
Product area namesCap Table, Due Diligence Pack, Company Manual
Modal titles — feature/screen namesData Room Settings, Add Employee
Table column headersDocument Name, Last Modified, Status
Tab labels / filter chipsUrgent, Proactive, Resolved
Short action CTAs (buttons)Send Invite, Mark Resolved, Review Unconfirmed Entries
Legal proper nouns (always)Cap Table, Shareholders' Agreement, Articles of Incorporation

Verb-first CTAs. Button labels always start with a verb: "Send Invite", not "Invite Send". Never noun phrases ("Invitation"), never ALL CAPS, never jargon ("Submit" → "Send", "Verify" → "Check", "Commence" → "Start").

Sentence case

Use when the copy explains something to the user. Capitalise only the first word and any proper nouns within the text.

Edge cases

Modal titles

Modal titles follow the content, not the chrome. A modal that opens a named feature ("Data Room Settings", "Add Employee") gets Title Case. A modal whose title is the insight description gets sentence case — the title describes an event, not a surface.

Modal purposeTitle exampleRegister
Named feature screenData Room SettingsTitle Case
Insight descriptionArticles of incorporation missing founder signaturesSentence case
Named action flowAdd EmployeeTitle Case

Legal proper nouns

Legal documents and commercial terms are proper nouns regardless of context. "Cap Table", "Shareholders' Agreement", "Articles of Incorporation", "Due Diligence Pack" are always Title Case — even when they appear mid-sentence in explanatory copy.

Correct: "Your Cap Table has unconfirmed entries" — "Cap Table" stays Title Case even though the rest is sentence case.

Badge labels

Badges are single words. The case distinction is moot — treat them as Title Case for consistency: "Active", "Stalled", "Complete", "Invited".

Loading and progress labels

Single-word progress indicators ("Resolving…", "Uploading…", "Saving…") are one word — no case decision needed. They are not CTAs; don't apply the verb-first rule.

Tone & voice

Calm authority. Steady and confident, never urgent. The product is a co-founder who happens to know the law — not a compliance system issuing warnings.

British English always. Authorised, organisation, licence (noun), colour, behaviour, defence. Body copy: 1–2 sentences maximum.

Research context

Major design systems — Material Design, Microsoft Fluent, PatternFly — recommend sentence case throughout for simplicity and localisation. LawAdvisor diverges intentionally for two reasons:

  1. Legal vocabulary includes genuine proper nouns. "Cap Table", "Shareholders' Agreement", "Articles of Incorporation" are named legal instruments. Sentence-casing them ("cap table") is grammatically incorrect in a professional context and would undermine the product's authority.
  2. The split-register rule is already implicit in the product. Navigation, tabs, and button labels have always used capitalised forms. Formalising the boundary — names vs explains — gives the team a rule to apply consistently rather than a pattern to guess at.

The primary risk of a split-register system is inconsistency. The mitigation is the rule itself: a single, answerable question ("does this name or explain?") that any designer or engineer can resolve without a style guide lookup.
